Loving, overtired, and isolated parents need to find a recommended local kid friendly event. TODAY!

SUMMARY

Merry Go is a mobile app that helps parents find today's kid friendly events and venues endorsed by local parents. It allows users to filter, search, and share events while connecting with those attending the event.

THE PROBLEM

Being a parent to young children is an exhausting and isolating endeavor. Finding a fun child focused activity is only a web search away. But how do you find recommended local kid friendly event and venues? The challenge is more than finding a destination it’s about answering parenting questions; Do they have changing tables? Is the performance during naptime? Can I use my stroller? Is there healthy food and drinks available? The best resources for answering these questions ironically are the collective intelligence of parents in the same situation.

THE SOLUTION

Merry Go is a mobile app that connect parents in their search for today's kid friendly event and venues. It allows users to filter, search, and share events while participating in a live forum about the event.

THE RESEARCH

Survey & Interview

I created a survey asking about the methods and resources people currently use to find local kid friendly events. I posted a link to the survey on a mother club online discussion forum and received 21 respondents. I also interviewed five survey respondents to clarify their answers and outline their pain points when looking for kid friendly events.

Personas

I conducted five impromptu interviews of caregivers at two local park playgrounds. I asked them about what resources they used to find local kid friendly events. I was shocked to discover that these caregivers relied on the bulletin board at a nearby cafés and the fellow playground caregivers for finding recommended local events. In addition, I also closely reviewed the survey respondent data, followed the Southern Marin Mothers list serve for a week and generally explored online parenting blogs. From this research, I created four user personas.

Competitive Analysis

The resource landscape for finding kid friendly event and activities is vast and overwhelming. From my survey and interview data, I created a basic SWOT analysis. My take away from this research is that people want context to their content, especially when it comes to which event they will take their children too. Here is a summary of what I found.

1. MOBILE APPS

If one were to combine these to mobile apps it would be Merry-Go. Big Tent offers the group forum platform and Yuggler offers the content search for kid friendly events. Currently, no mobile app contains both.

2. SOCIAL APPS


These social networking apps had weak functionality for parents looking for kid friendly events. The broad spectrum of offereing in these apps diliuted ease in finding kid friendly events.

3. WEBSITE


Many parents that I spoke with referred me to these sites when they searched for activties. They were good resource for large metropolitan regions. They offered several events and ideas with social network links. The question became how can parents not in big cities find what happening near by.

4. MOTHERS CLUBS



These popular sites that the bay area parents referenced were social networks that provided a comprehensive vetted list of local kid friendly events. The sticking point with these sites was the membership pay wall with a hyper regional focus.


5. BULLTIN BOARD

Yes, everyone interviewed mentioned this simple analog resource. These offices, library or café bulletin boards provided a snapshot of local kid friendly events within the social context of a contact person or neighborhood.

User Stories

After compiling a lengthy list of tasks that users would want to accomplish with my product, I prioritized those essential for a minimum viable product (edit profile, sorting, search, view, saving, etc) from the nice-to-haves (sharing linking, syncing, comment, adding and managing collaborators).

It is during this process that a fatal flaw was made but will not be revealed until the user testing. The mistake was how I catagorized the tasks. The tasked labeled here as "nice to have" are precisely those that users want most.

User Flows & Site Map

I converted the users stories into user flows to visualize how the user would accomplish those stories with my product. Separate flows demonstrate how new and returning users would be welcomed into the platform. A combined flow for tasks within a dashboard took several iterations as I mapped out the smoothest paths for adding content, searching for specific items, and managing accounts. To solidify our navigation structure and content hierarchy, I drew up a sitemap before diving into the design of the actual product.

BRANDING

I set out to create a visual design system for our product that would reflect the same professionalism while packing more visual punch.

Typography

The typography needed to reflect a modern predominantly female demographic. With her in mind, I found a rounded and fun Bree Serif font for the logo. To contrast the logo, I wanted some thing open, easily readable and san serif for the content font. I arrived at the tall and thin Poppins Light for headings and the ubiquitous Roboto for the body copy.

Brand Story & Logo

Early logo concepts combined playground, circus tents, wheels imagery to communicate the product’s function and key message. After further brainstorming and experimentation I selected the design around the playground theme because I could incorporate the idea of movement and fun.

When creating a brand I often begin with a kind of Socratic method to clarify the foundational elements behind the brand story. For this project, I began with the tag line to guide the story. Here is what a working Q & A looked like:

Q: What is this apps primary function?
A: Discovery of new places to go with kids. From this question, I wrote the tagline: “Let’s find some fun!”

Q: What is fun for both kids and parents?
A: Play

Q: Where do most children find play?
A: At a playground.

Q: What activity is foundational for discovering new places?
A: Movement

Q: What playground activity is iconic for movement?
A:The Merry- Go - Round and Swings

So the Merry –Go – Round became the name and brand icon because it signified both movement and play.

Colors

Searching key color indicators like fun, kids, vibrant, and feminine I found mostly cliché pinks, baby blues and lemon yellows that were either too pastel or over the top neon. I was looking for a more nuanced story for the Merry- Go color palate. Then I found these retro circus colors that contained the energetic fire engine reds and said “this is fun”.

KEY LEARNINGS

During testing, I discovered a key understanding about users that shifted the whole focus of this project. I learned that finding a kid friendly event didn’t resolve a caregiver’s feeling of isolation, which was a foundational premise of my initial prototype. What parents wanted most was an opportunity to socialize, connect and share with a community of people in similar circumstances. They longed to have a place to ask and answer questions, to get feedback and reconnect to the outside world. They wanted a social platform for mining the collective intelligence of the group that's events centric not location specific. This insight about the deeper psychological needs of the user revealed my assumptions and bias around this topic. This became clear for me when a user testing my prototype asked, "Where are the users?

Because I believe that documenting my work and honestly presenting the lesson's learned shows how the UX process works to build better products. It is a case study in how failure can reveal deep and powerful insight about the user. This new understanding has reinvigorated a new round of research for me. I have learned to looks out of the box of finding kid friendly events to how content search and social networks are linked. This journey has only just begun. Onward…
