Get strangers laughing and they are no longer strangers.

SUMMARY

Punch Line transforms telling jokes into a competitive sport that connects players everywhere. Pick a fight and send a joke with the punch line hidden. To reveal the punch line, your opponent must reply with a joke and the match begins. Boxers earn points and collect jokes by playing. Write a good joke and others will share it, while you earn points.

THE CHALLENGE

I was traveling on a train when another commuter suddenly bursts out laughing. Several people looked up with a grin at the laughing commuter and then went back to their screens. I wondered how an app could connect all those commuters with laughter.

THE SOLUTION

Design a mobile game that connects people and weaves a social humor network. Hear the laughter of a nearby person that you just threw a punch line at. Tell jokes at a party without taking the stage. Get strangers laughing and they are no longer strangers.

THE RESEARCH

Survey

I created a survey about mobile game apps and posted it on Reddit. My questionnaire focused mostly on the details around where and how people played mobile games. I summarized the data and came up with some common themes for user motivations, frustrations and considerations:

MOTIVATIONS

  • Users would often play games when they were waiting or alone
  • Chatting while playing was a common socialization tool of gamers.
  • Social circle of friends would gather to play games.

FRUSTRATIONS

  • Humor apps were never used in a social or gaming context.
  • Not being able to quickly respond to a game notification when not immediately playing
  • User had difficulty remembering good jokes.
  • Users rarely shared jokes while texting.
  • Funny introverts rarely had few opportunities to share their jokes.

CONSIDERATIONS

  • Games needed to accommodate different modality of play, such as playing game in short spurts.
  • Being known as a funny person was a social plus.
  • Being a gamer was often associated with socially awkward, introverted behavior.
  • A majority of female gamers especially enjoyed puzzle type games.

Personas

From my survey data and a series of interviews with self-proclaimed “mobile gamers”, I gained a deeper insight into the social world of gaming and the elements behind a successful game. I also came upon Richard Bartle prevalent player categorization models that reflected precisely what I found in my research. You can read abou it here: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartle_Test

Using this data and insights, I built four user personas.

User Stories & Flows

I discovered from my research that a successful game must be easy to learn and fun. This meant that user objective must be clear and that the user interface should be simple. BRANDING

The language of comedy has a violent vocabulary. You throw punch lines; you kill the audience, you bombed, slap stick, etc. The simple act of telling jokes is both fun and scary met with dramatic silence or visceral laughter. For these reasons, the game’s look and feel is founded on the display iconography of boxing. The brutally simple, black, white together with blood red color scheme combined with bold, tall fonts are iconic propaganda style reveal how linked laughter and combat truly are.

Logo

The logo came early in the process; I was collecting icons for a mock up and came across some boxing gloves vectors. I noticed one looked like it was smiling so I gave it some black eyes.

Color & Typography

The limited color scheme of black, red and yellow and usage of bold condensed san serif fonts came from my research of boxing posters.

EXECUTION

I was anxious to translate my notes and research into a working prototype so I could begin testing and refining the app. I built and tested numerous Sketch prototypes using INvision. I transformed barebones sketches into a clickable prototype through rounds of exploring visual design, conducting usability testing, and iterating on user feedback.

Prototype

My first attempt at creating a low fidelity prototype focused primarily on the mechanics of game play. I thought a swiping gesture mimicked throwing a punch. I also saw how intuitively this gesture worked with content drawers and designing for small screens. As outlined in the on boarding tutorial the user begins at their inbox where a group chat is under way. Then the user follows the 3 steps to begin the game.

  1. READY
    The user chooses which joker (in red) to respond by sending them a joke so to gather the punch line.

  2. AIM
    The user swipes left to reveal their joke database and chooses a joke. Swiping left again reveals the database filter.

  3. FIRE
    The user chooses a joke and taps it to send the joke.

I conducted three user tests that were done over the phone and using live share in Invision. Every user read the tutorial but did not understand drawers or the UI in general. I did learn that the users considered the swipe gesture not so much of an actionable gesture but primarily used it for sorting or browsing content. Overall the users echoed my user objectives precisily but this wasn't properly translated into a intuitive UI, so I went back to the drawing board.

  • User Testing Feedback R1

User Testing

It became clear that no one really understood the swiping gesture, they all wanted to hit a button in the middle of the screen. The brawl of jokes in the inbox most users found overwhelming. So in this version it was all about a one on one boxing matches. I also learned that sorting, writing and searching jokes was of paramount importance to players and game strategy. From these insights, I fundamentally changed the format of by dividing the screen into two boxes, the jokes and user on top and the chat match and opponent on the bottom. You can also see in this version how my brand boxing research influenced the prototype. The users take corners, user punch the central button, the winner belt, the colors and icons, etc.


These screens are at the end of a new user on-boarding flow. To easily find an opponent the location finder must be allowed. To enable a player to immediately begin fighting they are given two jokes. These jokes also provide home screen orientation and introduce users to how the game is scored.


Here is the home screen and the joke writing screen. The home screen is split in half with the upper part provided for the jokes database feed and the chat or match fight in the lower portion. To fire off a joke you use the red boxing glove on the separation line ¬¬on the middle right edge of the screen. The VS. button is used to find an opponent. The red plus is for writing jokes.


Every boxer needs an opponent and the VS button allows a player to start a fight. Boxers take their corners. Your user avatar and information are in the upper right red corner. Your competitor occupies the lower left corner.


The player who picks the fight gets the first punch line. The lower part of the screen becomes a chat arena or boxing ring. As players respond punch lines are revealed and collected.


ROUND TWO
From the feedback I received, I created a new prototype and then tested it in person with three new users. I also sent the new link to my first group of user and asked for their feedback. The testing revealed a better overall UI patterns but highlighted how game play demanded a further streamlined search and sorting functionality.

  • User Testing Feedback - R2

High Fidelity Prototype

From my user testing, I learned that thumbs are the masters of the game universe. So I accommodated those thumb wars and turned the screen horizontal. I also learned swiping is associated with browsing so in this version the user sorts and searches jokes by swiping.

The duality of the split screen was way to confusing of users. So instead of reinventing the wheel, I adopted the standard UI patterns for chat apps and how users send a gifs or emoji.

My study of gamification techniques greatly influenced this prototype. A more prominent score, leader boards, 12 round matches and time limits that influence scoring. Incentivizing starting fight by giving a point advantage. Punishing user with a counter punch if they send jokes their opponent has already heard. Overall, I focused on making the game fun and competitive without to many rules.

The on-boarding sequence also had to be reworked.

The high fidelity clickable prototype and a revised discussion guide served two purposes to gather more user feedback and as a tool for demonstrating the game for developers. After posting on Slack, I had several discussions with developers. These conversations revealed that I could continue to gather quantitative data from my clickable prototype and extrapolate that into game strategy and play. But the quickest and easiest way to gather true qualitative research meant making a simple working prototype to test the functionality and strategies of actual game play. So this is what I have currently began to do.

KEY LEARNINGS

I love doing research and gaming is such a rich and evolving area for exploring. Learning about gamification and the gamer psychology was fascinating. I also really enjoyed the passion of the "gamers" I interviewed and who tested my prototype.

Translating the idea of the game into a clear user interface was challenging. Done in solitude it felt like untangling a web. But with patience and applying the design thinking process (Empathize – Define – Ideate – Prototype - Test – Implement) I soon got a clear picture of the UI, and once I could see it, I could make it. Because my first profession was graphic design, my largest lesson was staying low fidelity for as long as possible. Design is a waist of time at the wire frame and lo fidelity level. Now I know!

User testing involves one of my favorite things, asking questions. And the insights from user testing were no less than revolutionary in clarifying user patterns and orienting the game. Through out this project, I have been amazed at the amount of enthusiasm Punch Line has inspired in others. After posting this project on Slack, I recieved lots of positive feed back and recently have been working with developers on building a working actionable game! I am very excited and look forward to sharing this once it is finished.
