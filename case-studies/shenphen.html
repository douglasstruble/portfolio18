SUMMARY

Punch Line transforms telling jokes into a competitive sport that connects players everywhere. Pick a fight and send a joke with the punch line hidden. To reveal the punch line, your opponent must reply with a joke and the match begins. Boxers earn points and collect jokes by playing. Write a good joke and others will share it, while you earn points.

THE CHALLENGE

I was traveling on a train when another commuter suddenly bursts out laughing. Several people looked up with a grin at the laughing commuter and then went back to their screens. I wondered how an app could connect all those commuters with laughter.

THE SOLUTION

Design a mobile game that connects people and weaves a social humor network. Hear the laughter of a nearby person that you just threw a punch line at. Tell jokes at a party without taking the stage. Get strangers laughing and they are no longer strangers.

THE RESEARCH

Survey

I created a survey about mobile game apps and posted it on Reddit. My questionnaire focused mostly on the details around where and how people played mobile games. I summerized the data and came up with some common themes for user motivations, frustrations and considerations:

MOTIVATIONS

  • Users would often play games when they were waiting or alone
  • Chatting while playing was a common socialization tool of gamers.
  • Social circle of friends would gather to play games.

FRUSTRATIONS

  • Humor apps were never used in a social or gaming context.
  • Not being able to quickly respond to a game notification when not immediately playing
  • User had difficulty remembering good jokes.
  • Users rarely shared jokes while texting.
  • Funny introverts rarely had few opportunities to share their jokes.

CONSIDERATIONS

  • Games needed to accommodate different modality of play, such as playing game in short spurts.
  • Being known as a funny person was a social plus.
  • Being a gamer was often associated with socially awkward, introverted behavior.
  • A majority of female gamers especially enjoyed puzzle type games.

Personas

I interviewed survey respondents and self-proclaimed “gamers” about their use of mobile games. From these interviews, I gained a deeper insight into the social world of gaming and further clarified elements that were behind a successful game. It was these interviews and survey data that informed the following four user personas.

User Stories

After compiling a long list of user stories for the game. I then prioritized the “nice to have” and “must have” functions for a minimum viable product. My persona research informed this process by prioritizing functionality based on game strategy, duration and frequency of play, and type of player.

Logos or game screen shots.

User Objectives

With my detailed task list and user research, I created a list of user objectives in the form of questions to help qualify my minimum viable product analysis.

User Flows

I created onboarding sequence for new and returning users with a tutorial about game play. The tutorial quantified the game play sequence as ready- aim – fire. Survey data points guided the user flow in the game design.

User Interface

Using my user flows, I drew up some wireframes on my white board and learned that I needed three basic screens: a boxing ring (a chat like experience), a joke database, and a user setting area. Inspired by the use of drawers on the Slack app, I I also wanted to incorporate a swiping gesture to send jokes and content drawers.

Mockups

Using my user stories, I sketched a basic mockup of the game. This process also helped me develop a more comprehensive user flow that incorporated both new and returning users. But with two user needs came more complexity, so I built a three-step walk through sequence to facilitate game play for new users.

BRANDING

The language of comedy has a violent vocabulary. You throw punch lines; you kill the audience, you bombed, slap stick, etc. The simple act of telling jokes is both fun and scary met with dramatic silence or visceral laughter. For these reasons, the game’s look and feel is founded on the display iconography of boxing. The brutally simple, black, white together with blood red color scheme combined with bold, tall fonts are iconic propaganda style reveal how linked laughter and combat truly are.

Logo

The logo came early in the process; I was collecting icons for a mock up and came across some boxing gloves vectors. I noticed one looked like it was smiling so I gave it some black eyes.

Color & Typography

The limited color scheme of black, red and yellow and usage of bold condensed san serif fonts came from my research of boxing posters.

Brand Story

The response to this branding concept of linking boxing publicity iconography with comedy has been both unanimously positive and easily understood.

EXECUTION

I transformed barebones sketches into a clickable prototype through rounds of exploring visual design, conducting usability testing, and iterating on user feedback.

Wire Frame

Drawing inspiration from the intuitive use of content drawers in Slacker’s UI and how a finger swipe gestures could mimic throwing a punch. I turned to post its and my whiteboard to create my wireframes.


I began with my user stories actions and grouped them together by tasks. Then I prioritized these task groups to the most common goals based on my user objectives being cautious of scope creep and focusing on a simple MVP, I arrived at three basic screens: a boxing ring (a chat like experience), a joke database, and a user setting area to accommodate these task groups and actions.

Low-Fidelity Protoype

I was anxious to translate my notes and research into a working prototype so I could begin testing and refining the app. I built and tested numerous Sketch prototypes using INvision.

High-Fidelity Prototype

From my copious user testing, I discovered two design insights; a horizontal screen orientation mimicked how users held a game controllers and the iMessage interface created an engaging and fun experience for the user. Further refinement of the user interface using color and branding choices also improved game. This high fidelity wireframe also inspired ideas notes for movement and other dynamic sequences that could be enhance game play. You can see a clickable prototype here.

Photographs in prototype used for simulation and analysis only. I do not own these images.

With the clickable prototype and a revised discussion guide, I conducted a second round of user testing focused on utility and usability. I captured feedback in an affinity map to categorize observations, synthesized our insights, and identifed key changes to improve usability going forward:

  • Users needed more visual cues related to active tabs and links. Our next iteration should ensure that all navigation elements follow standard placement and filtering tools reside in consistent locations.
  • Users wanted more feedback during interactions with forms. We should clarify default donation amounts and consider options for users to keep a copy of donations.
  • Since users weren’t sure about the meaning of “Featured” and the phrasing of Service Request instructions, subsequent revisions should replace ambiguous copy with precise terms and phrases.

Read my write-up about our second round of test results (4 minute read).

KEY LEARNINGS

I was surprised by the enthusiasm I received about this app. I also came to know and appreciate the U in UX and the power of user testing to quickly reveal all the bias and blind spots in a prototype built from a “good idea”. I learned that making a detailed user flow FIRST is key because it will save so much time during user testing. I also learned to stay big picture not get lost in the details, take copious notes, breath deep and not be afraid to start over.
