SUMMARY

This needs to be more than just the solution restated. Merry Go is a mobile app that finds todays kid friendly event and venues. Allows the user to filter, search share events and connect with others attending the event through users posts & comments.

THE PROBLEM

Loving, overtired, and isolated parents need to find a kid friendly, local event or fun destination, TODAY!.

THE SOLUTION

Merry Go is a mobile app that finds todays kid friendly event and venues. Allows the user to filter, search share events and connect with others attending the event through users posts & comments.

THE RESEARCH

Survey & Interview

I created a survey asking about the methods and resources people currently use to find local kid friendly events. I posted a link to the survey on a mother club online discussion forum and received 21 respondents. I also interviewed five survey respondents to clarify their answers and outline their pain points when looking for kid friendly events.

Personas

From my interviews, survey respondent data and study of the Southern Marin Mothers list serve and parenting blogs I created four personas.

Competitive Analysis

From my survey and interview data, I created a SWOT analysis and outlined the three types of resources caregivers typically use to find local kid friendly events.

APPS
The apps that the parents and caregivers said they used most fell into two basic categories.

Content Search - Yuggler & Yelp logo -
These search-based apps helped locate venues or family friendly events but had little community resources or calendaring options for parents of young children.


Content Search Social Networks - Next Door & FourSquare logo -
These social networking apps had weak functionality for parents looking for kid friendly events.

WEBSITE
The sites that the local caregivers referred me to fell into similar categories as the apps with one key difference being the social networks were location specific clubs behind a pay wall.

Content Search - Red Tricycle, Ronnie’s Awesome list & Everbrite -
Local mothers I spoke with referred me to these sites. They were good resource for large metropolitan regions that offered several events and ideas with social network links. The question became how can parents not in big cities find what happening near by.

Mother Clubs - Little Lane, Marin mothers club logo -
These popular sites that the bay area parents referenced were social networks that provided a comprehensive vetted list of local kid friendly events. The sticking point with these sites was the membership pay wall with a hyper regional focus.

BULLTIN BOARD
Yes, everyone interviewed mentioned this simple analog resource. These offices, library or café bulletin boards provided a snapshot of local kid friendly events within the social context of a contact person or neighborhood.

User Flows

I sketched out a short storyboard to help me ideate at a high level on how a user would interact with my product. I followed up with user flows to break down how the personas would travel the path from initial interaction to end goal. From these story boards I created a user story that outlined an on boarding sequence and search functions for showing local kid friendly events.

Feature Prioritization - Why don't I know how to do this?

The tasks portrayed in my user flows pointed me towards the next step to address our personas' needs: prioritizing features. I compiled a dream list of features and then narrowed it down using a 2x2 matrix. This revealed six high-impact features necessary to fulfill users' needs and that users would expect to see in our product.

Sitemap - This is vital to being a UX designer - yet don't know this well? Why?

To generate ideas for organizing our content, I conducted an open card sorting exercise to see how participants mentally classified pre-labeled cards and what terms they used to name their categories. Using the results, I drew up a sitemap to solidify the app's navigation structure and content hierarchy, sketching out a few versions before settling on a structure that would act as a reference point as I began to design the actual product.

BRANDING

I set out to create a visual design system for our product that would reflect the same professionalism while packing more visual punch.

Typography

The typography needed to reflect a modern predominantly female demographic. With her in mind, I found a rounded and fun Bree Serif font for the logo. To contrast the logo, I wanted some thing open, easily readable and san serif for the content font. I arrived at the tall and thin Poppins Light for headings and the ubiquitous Roboto for the body copy.

Brand Story & Logo

When creating a brand I often begin with a kind of Socratic method to clarify the foundational elements behind the brand story. For this project, I began with the tag line to guide the story. Here is what the working Q & A looked like:

Q: What is this apps primary function?
A: Discovery of new places to go with kids. From this question, I wrote the tagline: “Let’s find some fun!”

Q: What is fun for both kids and parents?
A: Play

Q: Where do most children find play?
A: At a playground.

Q: What activity is foundational for discovering new places?
A: Movement

Q: What playground activity is iconic for movement?
A:The Merry- Go - Round

So the Merry –Go – Round became the name and brand icon because it signified both movement and play.

Early logo concepts combined camera and file folder imagery to communicate the product’s function and key message. After further brainstorming and experimentation, the selected design consists of legal file folder subtly imitating a camera, paying homage to a commonplace item in law offices with just a hint of inventive humor.

Colors

Searching key color indicators like fun, kids, vibrant, and feminine I found mostly cliché pinks, baby blues and lemon yellows that were either too pastel or over the top neon. I was looking for a more nuanced story for the Merry- Go color palate. Then I found these retro circus colors that contained the energetic fire engine reds and said “this is fun”.

EXECUTION

Merry Go transformed from paper sketches into a clickable prototype through rounds of exploring visual design, conducting usability testing, and iterating on user feedback.

Paper Prototype

Using my user story and content search UI patterns, I pasted up a basic paper prototype and user tested them with neighbors and friends and collected feedback.

INvision Prototype- THis feels like a problem- paper protoypes jumping to INvision Prototype

Using my paper prototype and user feedback, I modeled a simpler version of the app in Sketch and built a working prototype with INvision. I tested this prototype with survey respondents and employed usertesting.com to gather further user feedback

.

INvision Prototype

WHY WASN"T I TAUGHT TO HALF OF THIS STUFF?

As I built my grayscale wireframes, I added annotations to imagine interactions between selected elements. I wanted to test which visual elements users would gravitate towards to understand which step they were accomplishing within a larger task. I also wanted to see if our category names and button labels corresponded to users' mental models. Using a screener, I recruited participants for in-person usability tests and guided them through interactive wireframes in InVision using a test script adapted from Steve Krug.

Testing revealed that users had no issues with submitting a document for eFiling when taking the task in order, but struggled when they were required to start in the middle of the task. This main user pain point informed my top action items to improve user experience:

  • Build breadcrumb and revise global navigation bar to better accomodate user flows;
  • Improve system feedback to provide textual or visual responses when users make selections (choose a filing fee) or do important actions (delete or submit documents); and
  • Consolidate screens or introduce modals to improve content organization and progressive disclosure.

High-Fidelity Prototype IN DON'T have this

Photofile came to life as I added color and iterated on my design to create a high fidelity, clickable prototype. I chose to apply a dark background to counteract the glare of harsh courtroom lights. Bright red was applied sparingly to point and condition users towards the location of primary actions.

KEY LEARNINGS

During testing, I discovered a key understanding about users that shifted the whole focus of this project. I learned that finding a kid friendly event didn’t resolve a caregiver’s feeling of isolation, which was a foundational premise of my initial prototype. What parents wanted most was an opportunity to socialize, connect and share with a community of people in similar circumstances, namely other parents. They longed to have a place to ask and answer questions, to get feedback and reconnect to the outside world. What is missing in most of the sites offering endless content for new parents is a social network component to connect all those isolated parents at local kid friendly events.

AND NOW…

With more time, I would conduct a third round of usability tests on my high-fidelity prototype. I would also conduct additional research into how our platform might partner with an existing eFiling vendor to break into the market, as well as the potential exposure to liability lawsuits.

I have also been looking further into what Big Tent is doing with its forum, event and classified platform. It seems to be leveraging local mother groups that use its platform to move into the mobile space. But its new app release has not been rated well and the content is dependent on the robustness of the mother club community.
